# Retail Operations Institute — Content System

An editorial system for publishing at McKinsey Insights / HBR quality: every
piece exists to demonstrate original, structured thinking about retail as a
system — not to summarize industry news or restate conventional wisdom.

---

## 1. Content Categories

Seven pillars. Every Insight is tagged to exactly one; frameworks and case
studies cross-link into whichever pillars they touch.

| Pillar | What it covers | Existing anchors |
|---|---|---|
| **Systems & Frameworks** | How to think in loops and constraints, not initiatives | Retail Flywheel Dynamics |
| **Store Operations & Execution** | Friction, standardization, planning process design | Operational Friction Index, Retail Operating Pyramid |
| **Workforce & Labor Strategy** | Scheduling, alignment, productivity decomposition | Labor-Capacity Alignment Model, Store Productivity Architecture |
| **Customer Experience & Store Design** | Journey stages, layout, flow | Customer Flow Optimization System |
| **Capital & Real Estate Strategy** | Remodels, construction, capital allocation discipline | Retail Capital Efficiency Loop, Retail Value Creation Matrix |
| **Data & Decision Making** | Metrics architecture, dashboard design, decision quality | Data-driven decision-making case study |
| **Executive Perspectives** | Short POV pieces on how retail leadership teams should think, written for a reader evaluating the author's judgment (recruiters, consulting partners, board members) | About page philosophy |

A pillar is a lens, not a folder wall — most strong pieces sit at the
intersection of two (a labor piece that's really about decision-making
architecture, for instance). Tag to the pillar the *argument* belongs to, not
the pillar the *subject matter* superficially belongs to.

## 2. Article Templates

Four repeatable shapes. Every new Insight picks one before a word is drafted —
the template is a thinking discipline, not a decoration applied afterward.

### A. The Diagnostic
*Names a problem readers have mis-diagnosed, then re-diagnoses it.*

1. Open with the conventional explanation (what most operators believe)
2. Show why that explanation doesn't hold up
3. Re-diagnose using a named framework
4. State what changes if the reader accepts the re-diagnosis
5. One-line closing reframe (quotable)

Best for: contrarian, high-share pieces. *Example: "Why Store Layout
Outperforms Signage Every Time."*

### B. The Framework Explainer
*Introduces or deepens a piece of original IP.*

1. Core idea in one sentence
2. The mechanism (how it actually works)
3. Where it breaks in practice (failure modes)
4. One applied example
5. What to do differently starting Monday

Best for: establishing proprietary vocabulary. This is the template the
`/frameworks` pages already follow structurally.

### C. The Executive Briefing
*Short, scannable, written for a reader with four minutes.*

1. The one number or fact that matters
2. Why it's surprising
3. The one decision it should change
4. Nothing else — no methodology digression, no caveats paragraph

Best for: LinkedIn-native distribution and recruiter/exec skimming.
Target length: 400–600 words.

### D. The Data Story
*A single chart or metric decomposition drives the entire argument.*

1. Show the chart or number first, before any argument
2. State what it would mean read naively
3. State what it actually means once decomposed
4. Name the framework that does the decomposing

Best for: pairing with the visual library (`/tools/visual-library`) — the
six charts already built are draft material for this template.

## 3. Writing Style Guide

**Voice**
- Third person or structural first person ("This piece argues," never "I
  think" or "In my opinion"). Authority comes from the argument's structure,
  not from asserting personal conviction.
- Present tense for mechanisms ("the loop accelerates"), past tense only for
  specific, dated observations.
- Active voice by default. Passive only when the actor is genuinely
  irrelevant to the point ("inventory records had drifted," not "someone had
  let inventory records drift").

**Sentence and paragraph discipline**
- One idea per sentence. If a sentence needs a semicolon to hold two claims,
  it's two sentences.
- Paragraphs stay under five sentences. A paragraph that runs longer is
  usually two arguments wearing one paragraph.
- Open every section with the claim, not the setup. The reader should be able
  to read only the first sentence of every paragraph and reconstruct the
  argument.

**Banned**
- Marketing language: "game-changing," "revolutionary," "unlock," "supercharge."
- Hedged authority: "it could be argued," "some might say," "arguably."
- Unsupported statistics. Every number is either sourced or explicitly marked
  **illustrative** — never presented as real data without that label.
- Rhetorical questions as section openers ("But what if there was a better
  way?"). State the claim instead.
- The em-dash as a crutch for weak sentence construction — reserve it for a
  genuine aside, not as a substitute for a period or comma.

**Required**
- Every original model gets its proprietary name on first use, linked to its
  `/frameworks` page.
- Every claim about "what most companies do" is falsifiable — phrased so a
  reader could disagree with a specific, checkable statement, not a vibe.
- A pull-quote-worthy closing line for every piece — one sentence that would
  read correctly lifted out of context onto a slide.
- Section headers are claims, not topics ("Execution Is Rarely the Layer
  That's Broken," not "About Execution").

## 4. SEO Strategy for Consulting / Recruiting Visibility

**Objective:** rank for searches a hiring manager, consulting partner, or
retail executive actually runs — not generic retail-blog traffic.

**Target query clusters**
- Framework-name queries: "retail flywheel framework," "operational friction
  index," "labor capacity alignment" — own these outright since the terms are
  proprietary; there is no incumbent competing for them.
- Role-adjacent queries: "retail operations systems thinking," "industrial
  engineering retail operations," "retail strategy consultant frameworks."
- Problem-adjacent queries: "why do new stores underperform," "retail capital
  allocation framework," "reduce retail labor friction" — these are the
  Diagnostic-template pieces' job.

**On-page mechanics**
- `seoTitle` / `seoDescription` fields (already in the content schema) are
  filled for every piece — never left to default to the on-page H1, since the
  SERP title can carry the framework's proprietary name even when the H1 is
  more conversational.
- One framework link and one case-study link, minimum, in every Insight body
  — this is the internal-linking mesh that makes the site read as a coherent
  body of work to both readers and crawlers, not a blog.
- URLs are the slug, not a date path — `/insights/why-store-layout...`, never
  `/insights/2026/04/18/...`. Evergreen framework content shouldn't look
  perishable.

**E-E-A-T signals (Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trust)**
- Consistent author identity (`sheldon-meeks`) across every framework,
  insight, and case study, with a stable bio — this is what turns "one more
  retail blog" into a recognizable byline.
- Case studies explicitly labeled illustrative vs. real (already implemented)
  — paradoxically *increases* trust signal quality, since it's the kind of
  precision a careless content farm doesn't bother with.
- Original frameworks with consistent proprietary names (™) signal genuine
  IP ownership to both readers and search engines' authorship models.

**Distribution (outside the site)**
- The Executive Briefing template is the LinkedIn-native format — post the
  400–600-word version natively, link back to the full Diagnostic/Framework
  piece for the reader who wants the mechanism.
- Every framework page is built to be the canonical destination for its own
  name — when the proprietary term is mentioned anywhere externally (a
  LinkedIn comment, a guest post), it should link to `/frameworks/[slug]`,
  compounding that page's authority.

**What this site deliberately does not do**
- No keyword-stuffed "best retail software 2026" comparison content — that
  targets commercial intent the Institute doesn't serve, and would dilute the
  systems-thinker positioning with listicle-adjacent content.
